Intro Data References Charts Findings

Introduction

For retailers, big sales numbers do not always mean the business is doing well. A category can sell a lot and still lose money because of discounts, shipping, or product mix. Our group wanted to focus on one main question: What affects profit in this dataset, and why do some parts of the business lose money even when sales look fine?

We use five charts that build on each other. First, an overview of sales and profit over time. Second, a scatter plot of discount vs profit on each order line. Third, bars to compare categories and regions. Fourth, a closer look at one bad segment (we picked something with high sales but weak profit). Fifth, an interactive D3 chart to dig into that segment. The idea is to go from the big picture down to one specific problem area.

What we had to do for class: clean and explore the data, make static charts in Altair and at least one interactive D3 chart, put everything on this site, and write up what we found.

This project matters because retail businesses often focus heavily on increasing sales without fully understanding how those sales translate into profit. By breaking down profit into its key drivers, we can identify inefficiencies in pricing strategies, product performance, and regional differences. The goal is not just to describe the data, but to highlight actionable patterns that could help improve decision-making in a real-world retail setting.

Data

We used the Superstore CSV from Kaggle (Rohit Sahoo, “Sales Forecasting”). It is a common classroom dataset: fake retail orders in the US from 2014 to 2017. We downloaded it as a CSV and loaded it in Python with pandas. Each row is one line item in an order (one product line), not one whole order.

After cleaning (dates parsed, duplicates removed if we found any, plus columns like year and month), we had about 9,800 rows and around 20 columns. Main fields include order date, ship date, ship mode, customer info, city/state/region, product category and subcategory, and numbers for sales, quantity, discount, and profit. There is a mix of categories and numbers, so we can do time series, bar charts, and scatter plots.

The dataset fits our topic because it has discount, region, and category, which are all things a store might care about for profit. It does not include real cost of goods, inventory, or marketing spend, so when we say “profit” we mean the profit column in the file. We cannot claim we proved causation from this alone, only patterns that match the story in the charts.

We chose this dataset because it provides a realistic view of retail operations while still being manageable for analysis. The combination of geographic, financial, and product-level variables allows us to connect multiple factors together rather than looking at profit in isolation. This makes it possible to explore how different variables interact and contribute to overall business performance.

Summary of findings

Putting the charts together: the time series shows overall performance; discount vs profit shows line-level risk; the bar charts show which regions and categories are weak; the subgroup chart names one concrete problem; the D3 piece explores that case. Exact numbers will depend on the final charts when they are plugged in.

Three main takeaways for our project question:

  1. Profit does not follow sales 1:1. Same sales level can mean different profit depending on discount and what was sold, so managers should look at margin and discount, not only revenue.
  2. Discounts need a second look in some segments. Where high discount and negative profit show up together, it is worth checking promotion rules or pricing, especially in categories that might have returns or high shipping (we cannot see all costs in the CSV, so this is still based on patterns in the data).
  3. Region and product type matter together. A bad region in one category might need a different fix than another region. We are not claiming we proved why, only what the data suggests for follow-up analysis.

If we had more time or data: add real cost of goods, returns, or longer time ranges, and maybe turn some of this into a simple dashboard for monthly checks.

Overall, our analysis shows that profitability is not driven by a single factor, but by the interaction between discounting strategies, product types, and regional differences. The consistent losses observed in specific subcategories suggest that certain business decisions may need to be reevaluated. These findings highlight the importance of using data visualization not just to report performance, but to uncover deeper insights that can guide better business strategies.
