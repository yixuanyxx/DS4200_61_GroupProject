Unboxing the Retail Pulse

Superstore sales, discounts, and what actually drives profit

Intro Data References Charts Findings

Introduction

For retailers, big sales numbers do not always mean the business is doing well. A category can sell a lot and still lose money because of discounts, shipping, or product mix. Our group wanted to focus on one main question: What affects profit in this dataset, and why do some parts of the business lose money even when sales look fine?

We use five charts that build on each other. First, an overview of sales and profit over time. Second, a scatter plot of discount vs profit on each order line. Third, bars to compare categories and regions. Fourth, a closer look at one bad segment (we picked something with high sales but weak profit). Fifth, an interactive D3 chart to dig into that segment. The idea is to go from the big picture down to one specific problem area.

This project matters because retail businesses often focus heavily on increasing sales without fully understanding how those sales translate into profit. By breaking down profit into its key drivers, we can identify inefficiencies in pricing strategies, product performance, and regional differences. The goal is not just to describe the data, but to highlight actionable patterns that could help improve decision-making in a real-world retail setting.

Data

We used the Superstore CSV from Kaggle (Rohit Sahoo, “Sales Forecasting”). It is a common classroom dataset: fake retail orders in the US from 2014 to 2017. We downloaded it as a CSV and loaded it in Python with pandas. Each row is one line item in an order (one product line), not one whole order.

After cleaning (dates parsed, duplicates removed if we found any, plus columns like year and month), we had about 9,800 rows and around 20 columns. Main fields include order date, ship date, ship mode, customer info, city/state/region, product category and subcategory, and numbers for sales, quantity, discount, and profit. There is a mix of categories and numbers, so we can do time series, bar charts, and scatter plots.

The dataset fits our topic because it has discount, region, and category, which are all things a store might care about for profit. It does not include real cost of goods, inventory, or marketing spend, so when we say “profit” we mean the profit column in the file. We cannot claim we proved causation from this alone, only patterns that match the story in the charts.

We chose this dataset because it provides a realistic view of retail operations while still being manageable for analysis. The combination of geographic, financial, and product-level variables allows us to connect multiple factors together rather than looking at profit in isolation. This makes it possible to explore how different variables interact and contribute to overall business performance.

Summary of findings

The five charts build on each other from a broad overview down to a specific loss pathway. Starting from the time series, it becomes clear that sales and profit do not move together. Revenue grows year over year, but profit margins remain thin and uneven. This sets up the central question: if sales are growing, why does profitability stay fragile? The remaining charts answer that question layer by layer.

Three main takeaways emerge from the combined analysis.

  1. Discounts above 20% reliably destroy profit. Across all categories, the median profit per order turns negative once discounts exceed 30%, and virtually no orders with discounts above 35% are profitable. Furniture is hit hardest, as its interquartile range falls entirely below zero at high discount levels, meaning the majority of heavily discounted Furniture orders lose money, not just the outliers. The D3 Sankey makes this pathway visible, showing that Standard Class shipping combined with discounts above 20% is the single widest loss flow in the entire dataset. Reducing deep discounts in Furniture, particularly in loss-heavy states like Texas and Ohio, could be the highest-impact lever for improving overall margins.
  2. Losses are geographic and product-specific, not random. Texas, Ohio, and Pennsylvania account for a disproportionate share of total losses, particularly in Furniture sub-categories like Tables and Bookcases. Tables alone generate nearly $18,000 in cumulative losses despite over $200,000 in sales, a stark sign that the problem is structural rather than incidental. California and New York, by contrast, generate profit even in otherwise weak categories, suggesting that regional pricing behavior or customer mix differs meaningfully across the country. A uniform national discount policy is unlikely to serve all markets equally well.
  3. Ship mode has a measurable but secondary effect. Second Class shipping yields the highest average profit margin, roughly 15 to 19 percent depending on category, while Standard Class, used in about 60% of all orders, earns only around 11%. This gap is consistent across all three product categories. However, ship mode appears to amplify the discount problem rather than cause it independently, as the worst outcomes in the Sankey diagram occur when Standard Class and high discounts combine, not when either factor appears alone. Encouraging customers toward Second Class for mid-size orders could improve margins, but it would not fix the underlying discount structure.

Future directions

The current dataset captures transaction-level outcomes but cannot fully explain why certain products or regions are systematically unprofitable. Three categories of additional data would sharpen the analysis considerably.

  • Actual logistics cost. The dataset assigns a ship mode label to each order but records no freight cost. A heavy Furniture item like a conference table and a box of pens can both ship Standard Class, yet their actual shipping costs differ by an order of magnitude. Without per-order freight data, it is impossible to separate the cost of delivery from the cost of discounting as drivers of margin loss. Real logistics data would also clarify whether the ship mode gap in profit margin reflects a genuine efficiency difference or simply a selection effect, as customers who choose Second Class may also be the ones who request fewer discounts.
  • Itemized cost of goods. The profit column in the dataset is a net figure that may already embed inconsistent cost assumptions across categories. Technology products, for example, often carry higher insurance and specialized packaging requirements that may not be captured uniformly. True gross margin data broken down by sub-category would allow a cleaner comparison and would help identify whether Furniture losses stem from low selling prices, high fulfillment costs, or both.
  • Return and refund records. Some of the largest negative profit values in Furniture, particularly in Tables, may reflect post-sale adjustments rather than pure pricing losses. Without return data, it is hard to tell whether a loss on a table order represents a deeply discounted sale or a returned item that was never restocked successfully. Separating these two cases matters because the interventions are completely different. One calls for discount caps and the other calls for return policy reform.

With these three additions, it would be possible to build a profitability model that separates product margin from logistics cost and post-sale leakage, giving managers a much clearer picture of where a single dollar of intervention would have the greatest impact.
