Unboxing the Retail Pulse

Superstore sales, discounts, and what actually drives profit

Intro Data References Charts Findings

Introduction

For retailers, big sales numbers do not always mean the business is doing well. A category can sell a lot and still lose money because of discounts, shipping, or product mix. Our group wanted to focus on one main question: What affects profit in this dataset, and why do some parts of the business lose money even when sales look fine?

We use five charts that build on each other. First, an overview of sales and profit over time. Second, a scatter plot of discount vs profit on each order line. Third, bars to compare categories and regions. Fourth, a closer look at one bad segment (we picked something with high sales but weak profit). Fifth, an interactive D3 chart to dig into that segment. The idea is to go from the big picture down to one specific problem area.

This project matters because retail businesses often focus heavily on increasing sales without fully understanding how those sales translate into profit. By breaking down profit into its key drivers, we can identify inefficiencies in pricing strategies, product performance, and regional differences. The goal is not just to describe the data, but to highlight actionable patterns that could help improve decision-making in a real-world retail setting.

Data

We used the Superstore CSV from Kaggle (Rohit Sahoo, “Sales Forecasting”). It is a common classroom dataset: fake retail orders in the US from 2014 to 2017. We downloaded it as a CSV and loaded it in Python with pandas. Each row is one line item in an order (one product line), not one whole order.

After cleaning (dates parsed, duplicates removed if we found any, plus columns like year and month), we had about 9,800 rows and around 20 columns. Main fields include order date, ship date, ship mode, customer info, city/state/region, product category and subcategory, and numbers for sales, quantity, discount, and profit. There is a mix of categories and numbers, so we can do time series, bar charts, and scatter plots.

The dataset fits our topic because it has discount, region, and category, which are all things a store might care about for profit. It does not include real cost of goods, inventory, or marketing spend, so when we say “profit” we mean the profit column in the file. We cannot claim we proved causation from this alone, only patterns that match the story in the charts.

We chose this dataset because it provides a realistic view of retail operations while still being manageable for analysis. The combination of geographic, financial, and product-level variables allows us to connect multiple factors together rather than looking at profit in isolation. This makes it possible to explore how different variables interact and contribute to overall business performance.

Summary of findings

Putting the charts together: the time series shows overall performance; discount vs profit shows line-level risk; the map shows which states and categories are weak; the subgroup chart examines ship mode and profit margin; and the D3 piece lets users explore the discount-to-loss pipeline interactively.

Three main takeaways for our project question:

  1. Discounts above 20% reliably destroy profit. Across all categories, the median profit per order turns negative once discounts exceed 30%, and virtually no orders with discounts above 35% are profitable. Furniture is hit hardest: its IQR falls entirely below zero at high discount levels. Reducing deep discounts in Furniture could be the single highest-impact lever for improving margins.
  2. Losses are geographic and product-specific, not random. Texas, Ohio, and Pennsylvania account for a disproportionate share of losses, particularly in Furniture sub-categories like Tables and Bookcases. California and New York, by contrast, generate profit even in otherwise weak categories. This suggests regional pricing or discount policies may need to be differentiated.
  3. Ship mode choice has a measurable but secondary effect. Second Class shipping yields the highest average profit margin (~15–19%), while Standard Class, used in 60% of all orders , earns roughly 11%. Encouraging customers toward Second Class for mid-size orders could improve margins without requiring changes to product pricing or discount policy.

Future directions: The current dataset captures what happened at the transaction level, but it does not explain why certain products or regions are unprofitable. Several additional data sources would make a meaningful difference. First, actual shipping cost per order would allow us to distinguish between the time-based definition of ship mode (Standard, First Class, etc.) and the real financial cost of moving each item. A heavy Furniture piece like a conference table likely incurs far higher freight charges than a lightweight Office Supply item shipped under the same "Standard Class" label, yet the dataset treats them identically. Second, itemized cost data including manufacturing cost, packaging, and insurance would let us compute true gross margin rather than relying on the profit column, which may already reflect some of these costs inconsistently. Technology products, for example, often carry higher insurance and specialized packaging requirements that are not visible in the current data. Third, return and refund records would help identify whether some of the large negative profit values in Furniture reflect actual pricing losses or post-sale adjustments. With these additional dimensions, it would be possible to build a more granular profitability model, one that separates product-level margin from logistics cost, and gives managers a clearer picture of where intervention would have the greatest impact.

Overall, our analysis shows that profitability is not driven by a single factor. It depends on the interaction between discounting strategies, product types, and regional differences. The consistent losses observed in specific subcategories suggest that certain business decisions may need to be reevaluated. These findings highlight the importance of using data visualization not just to report performance, but to uncover deeper insights that can guide better business strategies.
